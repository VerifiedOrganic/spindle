# Voice differentiation guide

This reference helps you keep characters from collapsing into one shared prose
voice.

## Build the voice signal

Define voice using choices the reader can actually hear.

- Vocabulary: what words this character reaches for.
- Syntax: how long or short their sentences run.
- Rhythm: where they pause, interrupt, or pile on.
- Metaphor domain: what comparisons they naturally make.
- Social stance: whether they hedge, command, deflect, or provoke.

## Contrast characters deliberately

Different voices are easiest to maintain when the contrast is structural.

- Give one character compression and another sprawl.
- Give one character concrete language and another abstract language.
- Give one character direct conflict and another evasive indirection.
- Give one character formal diction and another idiom-heavy speech.

## Test during revision

Use these checks when dialogue starts sounding interchangeable.

- Remove dialogue tags and see if you still know who is speaking.
- Read only one character's lines in sequence and check for consistency.
- Compare word choice across scenes for repeated generic phrasing.
- Check whether stress changes rhythm without erasing identity.

## Guardrails

Keep voice distinct without turning characters into caricatures.

- Avoid catchphrases as the only differentiator.
- Let voice evolve with status, intimacy, and fatigue.
- Keep narration aligned with POV without flattening every POV into the same
  authorial cadence.
