# Swain scene and sequel guide

This reference turns Dwight Swain's scene and sequel structure into a compact
planning and revision tool for Spindle workflows.

## Scene structure

A scene usually runs on pressure and collision.

- Goal: what the POV character wants right now.
- Conflict: what blocks that goal.
- Disaster: the turn that leaves the situation worse, not cleaner.

If the disaster does not force adjustment, the scene is probably ending too
soft.

## Sequel structure

A sequel lets the character metabolize what just happened before the next push.

- Reaction: the emotional or physical aftershock.
- Dilemma: competing choices or costs.
- Decision: the commitment that launches the next scene.

## Chapter rhythm

You do not need a perfect one-to-one alternation, but you do need recovery and
recommitment somewhere in the sequence.

- Stack scenes to accelerate pressure.
- Use sequels to reveal cost and sharpen intent.
- Shorten sequels when momentum is already clear.
- Lengthen sequels when a turn changes identity, allegiance, or worldview.

## Revision checks

When a chapter drags, inspect these failure modes first.

- The goal is weak or vague.
- The conflict is repetitive.
- The disaster does not change the next decision.
- The sequel repeats emotion without producing a plan.
