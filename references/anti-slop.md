# Anti-slop reference

This reference gives you a practical rewrite checklist for prose that feels
generic, inflated, or machine-assembled. Use it during drafting, revision, and
dual-persona review.

## What to cut first

Start by removing language that does not change meaning or sharpen the reader's
 experience.

- Cut abstract intensifiers like `suddenly`, `somehow`, `very`, and `deeply`
  unless they carry a specific voice signal.
- Cut summary phrases that repeat what the reader already understands.
- Cut ornamental synonyms that flatten character voice.
- Cut placeholder emotion labels when the scene can show behavior instead.

## Common patterns

Use this list as a fast scan during line edits.

- Repeated sentence openings in back-to-back paragraphs.
- Dialogue that explains subtext instead of carrying it.
- Generic body-language fillers such as shrugging, nodding, or sighing in every
  exchange.
- Empty urgency markers such as `everything changed` without concrete change.
- Expository recap inside scene action.
- Scene endings that restate the conflict instead of shifting it.

## Rewrite moves

When a paragraph feels flat, replace explanation with one of these moves.

- Swap abstraction for one sensory detail tied to POV.
- Replace a generic verb with a precise action.
- Compress two weak lines into one line with consequence.
- Turn a declarative statement into a choice, interruption, or refusal.
- Make one sentence carry both emotion and story movement.

## Final pass

Before saving a draft, confirm the prose passes these checks.

- Each paragraph moves action, emotion, or tension.
- Each named character sounds recognizably like themself.
- Scene summary language does not leak into the prose.
- The final line changes expectation, pressure, or desire.
